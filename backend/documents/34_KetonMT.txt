Ketogenics and
Metabolic Therapy
The ketogenic diet, often referred to as “keto,” is a high-fat, low-carbohydrate diet that has gained significant attention for its potential role in metabolic therapy, particularly in cancer treatment. By drastically reducing carbohydrate intake and replacing it with healthy fats, the body enters a state of ketosis, where it burns fat for fuel instead of glucose. This metabolic shift not only supports overall health but also inhibits the growth of cancer cells by depriving them of their primary energy source. The ketogenic diet’s unique approach to nutrition offers promising benefits for those seeking alternative or complementary therapies in their cancer treatment journey.

Understanding ketosis
Why is it SO Important?
Ketosis is a natural metabolic state where the body shifts from using glucose as its primary energy source to burning fat for fuel. This process occurs when carbohydrate and sugar intake is significantly reduced, leading to lower insulin levels and increased fat breakdown. The liver then converts fatty acids into ketones, which serve as an alternative energy source for the brain and most other cells and organs. This metabolic adaptation not only supports weight loss and improved energy levels but also offers potential therapeutic benefits for various health conditions, including epilepsy, type 2 diabetes, and cancer.

Fatty Acid Conversion
As glycogen stores are depleted, the liver begins converting fatty acids into ketones. These ketones serve as an alternative energy source for the brain and other organs, replacing glucose.

Transitioning to Ketosis
Getting Started
Understanding the stages of transitioning to ketosis can help ease the process and ensure a smoother adaptation. By gradually reducing carbohydrates, increasing fat intake, and allowing the body time to adapt, individuals can effectively achieve and maintain ketosis. This section will guide you through the essential steps and considerations for a successful transition to a ketogenic diet.

Initial Reduction of Carbohydrates
Begin by significantly reducing carbohydrate intake to around 20 grams per day. This reduction forces the body to deplete its glycogen stores and start utilising fat for energy.

Increased Fat Intake
Replace the reduced carbohydrates with healthy fats, such as avocados, nuts, seeds, olive oil, and animal fats (our preferred source). This helps the body transition to using fat as its primary fuel source.

Gradual Adaptation
Allow your body time to adapt to the new metabolic state. This may involve experiencing some initial side effects, such as the “keto flu,” which can include symptoms like headaches, fatigue, and irritability. Staying hydrated and maintaining electrolyte balance can help ease this transition.

Monitoring Ketosis
You can't master what you don't measure.​
Monitoring ketosis is essential to ensure that your body remains in this beneficial metabolic state. By regularly checking ketone levels and tracking the Glucose Ketone Index (GKI), you can make informed adjustments to your diet and lifestyle to maintain ketosis.

Measuring Ketone Levels
Use blood tests, urine strips, or breath analysers to measure ketone levels. Blood tests are the most accurate, while urine strips and breath analysers offer convenient alternatives.

Tracking Glucose Ketone Index (GKI)
Calculate the GKI by dividing your blood glucose level by your blood ketone level. This index provides a comprehensive view of your metabolic state and helps you monitor your progress.

Adjusting Diet and Lifestyle
Based on your ketone levels and GKI, make necessary adjustments to your diet and lifestyle to maintain ketosis. This may involve tweaking your macronutrient ratios, increasing physical activity, or managing stress levels.
